Pocket Casts

Pocket Casts has been my go-to mobile podcast client for the past several years. It was among the first Android apps to implement Google's Material Design, and it's small price tag meant there were no in-app advertisements. It gets a lot of things right, but there are some areas that could be more intuitive.

I’ve focused on the “Filters” tab. When selected, you're greeted with a list of filters. Tapping on a given filter will present the user with podcasts and episodes that meet the criteria of their desired filter. Upon closer inspection, the filter setting organization can be confusing. In the provided filters, you would think that the "Downloaded" filter and its universal download icon would return... well... downloaded podcasts. That is not always the case. The app ships with a set of predefined filters. Those filters, however, are mutable, meaning you can alter the downloaded filter to include podcasts that don't actually exist locally. In my personal user experience, I must’ve changed the parameters of the predefined download filter at some point and forgotten, leaving me very confused when some podcasts in my downloaded list were not actually downloaded. It's part user error, but I figured out a way to eliminate the potential for user error and subsequent frustration. To remedy this, I would disallow users from editing obvious filters such as “Downloaded” and “In Progress,” and reserve the filter screen for editing and creating custom filters.

In the existing version of the app, when you decide which filter you want to apply or edit, you're met with a screen containing your filtered podcasts, and a strip on top that lets you edit said filter, making the screen multi-purpose. You can edit and view filtered podcasts. I’ve removed podcasts from this screen to yield two improvements: simplifying the information displayed and eliminating unnecessary user confusion.

It frees up space for filter options to be presented in a cleaner fashion with less confusing language. In my redesign, by dedicating the entire screen to filter options, I was able to use language that was more clear. As you can see below, instead of the ambiguous “Unplayed, In Progress” chip at the top of the screen in the current version, I was able to create a list with three separate checkboxes, one for each available option.

In addition, by including podcasts in the filter screen, it has the potential to be unclear what’s actually being filtered. Am I filtering just the podcasts I'm subscribed to? Or, the entire Pocket Casts catalog? By eliminating podcasts from this screen, you subsequently eliminate confusion.

Since the filter tab no longer returns podcasts, your custom filters can be applied on the "My Podcasts" screen. Here, you can apply your custom filters by tapping the new filter icon at the top of the screen. To deselect a filter, either collapse the filters by tapping on the icon again, or, by tapping the "X" on the active filter. Here is what the new screens looks like:

Selecting a filter will hide the image tiles for the podcast that does not meet the selected filter requirements. This cleans up the filter tab to only show content relevant to creating and editing filters, making the purpose of both the "My Podcasts" and "Filters" tabs more explicit.

By streamlining each screen, I was able to improve user experience by creating a more fluid, intuitive, and simple design that eliminates the risk of common user error and unnecessary frustration.

Golden Hour

Golden Hour is a mobile app that lets you view the time and duration of different phases of light. It's extremely useful in nature and landscape photography. The application has all the necessary features, is free, and contains no ads. Some actions, however, could be easier to find or perform. Let's start by taking a look at the main screen, which they call “The Grid.”.

This is what greets the user when the app starts up. It’s more of a chart than a grid. At first glance, it takes a minute to figure out exactly what you're looking at because of the lack of headers. The screen is cluttered with some information that isn't especially useful (such as the UTC offset and exact latitude and longitude of my selected location). Perhaps there are some circumstances under which this is necessary, but in most cases, it’s distracting more than anything.

Certain navigation in the app could be more intuitive. For instance, finding the map screen takes a little exploring. In order to get to the map, you need to tap the menu dots at the top right of the screen and select "Map" from the context menu. Given that this view is equally as important as the grid for determining things such as when the sun will hit the face of a certain building, it should be easier to see.

Golden Hour 'Grid' Screen
Golden Hour 'Map' Screen

The last area I see for improvement, again, has to do with the map view. In order to see the trajectory of the sun on the map, your pin has to be in the middle of your point of interest. In order to change your POI, you need to tap and hold the pin to move it, which makes it hard to position it just right. In a perfect world, the pin should stay centered as you navigate the map and offer a button to recenter it.

I have solved the three above issues by stripping away non-essential information from the grid view, adding headers, improving the navigation through the addition of a navbar for easy switching between the grid and map view, and by simplifying the map. As another improvement, I have also developed an additional screen that allows the user to select their desired light based off of a gradient scale. The new screen creates a color visualization of the phases of light, letting the user tap the desired color. Once the desired color is selected, information about that phase is displayed at the top of the screen. The first image depicts my changes to the grid, and the second one is a sketch of the new screen I'd like to introduce.

A User Viewing the New Grid
Redesigned Golden Hour Sketch

As you can see, the new grid is less ambiguous than the original and the top of the display is less cluttered. As you can see on the redesign, the grid tab will now include a button that lets the user toggle this new view. When opened, the gray line depicts the current time. Tapping or dragging anywhere on the scale moves the red line out, and information about that phase appears on the top of the screen. Tapping the reset icon sets the display back to the current time. At the top of the screen, you can see the time of sunrise, sunset, current time, the start of the current phase, end of the current phase, and the duration of the current phase.

In alignment with my other improvements, I also updated the aesthetic of the settings tab to match the rest of my redesign.

Omcom

Omcom is an application that I designed myself, but have yet to implement. It is a chat-based service that functions in a similar fashion to a ride sharing app.

Omcom Logo

Today, smartphones are ubiquitous in many parts of the world. Which means there are billions of cameras floating around our streets, restaurants, hiking trails, and beyond. Omcom aims to provide a one-to-many video streaming platform that connects users to cameras in places they want to see. Are you curious how the traffic looks on your morning commute? If there's a table at that hip restaurants you like that doesn't take reservations? What the trail conditions are like? Omcom is your window to the world!

Users can connect or start sharing instantly without the need to sign in or create an account, shortening the barrier to entry and getting more active users on the platform. Tapping the connect button will bring you to a map showing users who are either currently streaming or ready to stream.

A host's activity level is denoted by the color of their pin on the map.

JointDJ

JointDJ is a unique webapp that features a desktop and mobile interface. The goal of the application is to function as a jukebox, allowing multiple users to curate a shared, live playlist.

It works like this: Someone creates an event. A unique, human readable code is generated for that event. Then, anyone with the code can navigate to the player for that event. Multiple users can open the player separately on their own computers, or, the event can be displayed on a shared screen such as a TV using a Chromecast or similar device. The player prominently displays the event code, so if the application is used in a public setting, anyone can add songs using their smartphone. Anyone with the code can manipulate the player, and different privileges can be granted or revoked by the user who created the event.

Since most users adding to the playlist will be using a smartphone, the controller interface is implemented in a mobilefirst framework (Ionic) that responds well to tapping, dragging, and swiping.
