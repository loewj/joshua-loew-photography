photography

It’s hard to pinpoint when I first expressed an interest in photography. I remember having a few disposable cameras throughout my youth, and picking up my father’s DSLR for the first time to experiment with the different shooting modes in my early teens. Once I discovered manual mode, I was fascinated by each setting’s impact on the final image.

I continued to learn the basics and photograph on and off for the next decade or so, but never in a serious capacity. As I got older, spending more time outdoors became important to me. I began to notice more subtle beauty around me, and that catalyzed my love affair with nature and landscape photography. I was eager to go out on walks with my camera around my neck. My love for the outdoors and photography grew as one unified passion; I interchangeably use one as an excuse to partake in the other. This began my extensive (almost obsessive) research of the process behind nature and landscape photography and its community.

I am inspired by historical greats and contemporaries such as Nick Carver, Alex Burke, Ben Horne, and David Brookover. As recently as a few years ago, I ignorantly viewed film photography as a relic of the past. Nothing more than expensive, kitschy, and outdated. Now, with the help and encouragement of other photographers, I recognize just how special it is. It requires devotion and patience. While digital cameras still demand care when it comes to subject and composition, they obscure much of what happens after the shutter is released. While the scientific process of film is magical, it’s far from mysterious. If you put in the time and effort to understand each step along the way from unexposed film, to latent image, to finished photograph, it’s an incredibly rewarding medium.

Film photography allows me to venture into the wilderness without having to charge batteries or look at LCD screens. It allows me to work with different formats and gorgeous color palettes unrivaled by digital cameras. It allows me to divorce myself from the scene between exposing the frame and viewing the processed inverted negative or positive. Most importantly, it forces me to slow down and consider the scene before me, how the changing light affects it, what settings I should use to achieve my creative vision, and why this particular place and time is worthy of photographing.

Thanks for stopping by, and I hope you enjoy my work!
